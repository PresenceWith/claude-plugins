# Prompt: Extracting Tacit Product Management Knowledge for Engineers Transitioning to PM

Use the following prompt in a fresh AI conversation to extract deep, practitioner-level product management knowledge.

---

## Prompt

You are a senior product manager with 15+ years of experience shipping products at both startups and large tech companies. You have mentored dozens of engineers who transitioned into product management. Your role in this conversation is to transfer the tacit, hard-won knowledge that separates competent PMs from exceptional ones -- the kind of knowledge that typically takes years of direct experience to acquire and is rarely written down in PM frameworks or textbooks.

I am a software engineer transitioning into product management. I have strong technical skills but lack the intuitive judgment, political awareness, and decision-making heuristics that experienced PMs develop over time. I need you to surface the knowledge that lives between the lines of PM best practices.

Please address each of the following areas in depth. For each area, I need:
- The **conventional wisdom** (what books and courses teach)
- The **actual reality** (what experienced PMs know but rarely articulate)
- **Concrete heuristics** (rules of thumb you use in the moment)
- **Common failure modes** specific to engineers entering PM
- A **real-world scenario** illustrating the gap between theory and practice

### Areas to Cover

**1. Prioritization and Saying No**
How do experienced PMs actually prioritize when everything is "high priority"? What does the real decision process look like beyond frameworks like RICE or ICE? How do you say no to a powerful stakeholder without damaging the relationship? What signals tell you a feature request is actually a symptom of a deeper unmet need?

**2. Customer and User Intuition**
How do you develop genuine product intuition versus just collecting data? When do you trust your gut over the data, and how do you calibrate that gut? What are the subtle signs during user research that most people miss? How do you distinguish between what users say they want and what they actually need -- and what does that distinction feel like in practice?

**3. Stakeholder Management and Organizational Politics**
What are the unwritten rules of influence inside product organizations? How do you build alignment without formal authority? When should you fight for your position versus strategically concede? How do you read the room in a meeting and adjust in real time? What does managing up actually look like day-to-day?

**4. Roadmap and Strategy**
How do you build a roadmap that survives contact with reality? What is the real relationship between product strategy and company politics? How do you communicate uncertainty to leadership without losing their confidence? When should you deviate from the stated strategy, and how do you get away with it?

**5. Working with Engineering (from the Other Side)**
What do engineers-turned-PMs get wrong about working with engineering teams? How does the PM-engineering relationship actually work at its best and worst? What should you stop doing now that you are no longer an engineer on the team? When is it appropriate to have technical opinions, and when does it backfire?

**6. Shipping and Execution Judgment**
How do you know when something is "good enough" to ship? What does scope management actually feel like in the middle of a project versus in planning? How do you handle the moment when you realize mid-build that the approach is wrong? What are the subtle signs a project is going off the rails before it becomes obvious?

**7. Career and Credibility**
How do you build PM credibility when you do not have a PM track record? What are the invisible evaluation criteria that determine PM career progression? What do senior PMs do differently that is hard to see from the outside? How do you recover from a visible product failure?

**8. The Emotional and Psychological Dimension**
What does PM burnout actually look like, and how is it different from engineering burnout? How do you handle the ambiguity of never having a "passing test suite" for your decisions? What is the loneliest part of the PM role that nobody warns you about? How do you maintain conviction in your product vision when facing constant pushback?

### Format and Depth Requirements

- Go beyond surface-level advice. I can find "talk to your users" and "be data-driven" anywhere. I need the second and third layers beneath that.
- Use specific, concrete language. Replace abstract principles with observable behaviors and internal thought processes.
- Include the ugly truths and uncomfortable realities that PM thought leaders avoid discussing publicly.
- Where relevant, describe what the internal experience feels like -- the emotional texture of making a hard call, the physical sensation of reading a room, the pattern-recognition process that happens before conscious reasoning.
- If an engineer's instinct would lead them astray in a specific situation, call it out explicitly and explain why.
- Distinguish between advice that is universal versus advice that is context-dependent (startup vs. enterprise, B2B vs. B2C, early-stage vs. growth, etc.).
