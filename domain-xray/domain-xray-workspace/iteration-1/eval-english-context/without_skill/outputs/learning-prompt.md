# Deep Product Management Knowledge Extraction Prompt

## Context for the AI

I am a software engineer transitioning into my first Product Manager role, which starts in approximately one month. I have strong technical skills (architecture, system design, code-level thinking, debugging, shipping software) but limited formal PM experience. I need you to act as a senior PM mentor with 15+ years of experience across startups and large tech companies. Your goal is to transfer the **tacit knowledge** -- the unwritten, experience-based expertise that PM veterans carry but rarely articulate in books or courses.

Do not give me textbook definitions. I already know how to read a blog post. Instead, give me the **insider playbook**: the things I would only learn after years of painful trial and error.

---

## Part 1: The Hidden Mental Models

Explain the mental models that elite PMs actually use day-to-day but rarely write about. For each mental model:

1. **Name it** concisely.
2. **Describe the real-world situation** where it applies (not a hypothetical -- describe it as if recounting what happened at a real company).
3. **Explain why an engineer-turned-PM would get this wrong** at first.
4. **Give me the corrected instinct** -- the way a veteran PM would handle it.

Cover at least the following areas:
- Deciding what NOT to build (and how to say no without burning bridges)
- Knowing when data supports a decision vs. when data is being used as a shield to avoid making a decision
- Reading the room in a cross-functional meeting and knowing whose concerns are real blockers vs. performative objections
- The difference between user requests and user needs (and how to extract the latter from the former)
- Managing upward: how to present to executives so your roadmap survives contact with leadership
- Knowing when a product is "good enough" to ship vs. when the remaining bugs will destroy trust

---

## Part 2: The First 90 Days -- What Actually Matters

Walk me through the first 90 days as a new PM, but not the sanitized version from onboarding guides. Tell me:

1. **Week 1-2: The intelligence-gathering phase**
   - Who should I talk to first, and what specific questions draw out honest information rather than corporate-speak?
   - What artifacts should I read, and what should I read *between the lines* of those artifacts?
   - How do I identify the real decision-makers vs. the nominal ones?
   - What are the warning signs that a team/product has hidden dysfunction?

2. **Week 3-4: Establishing credibility without overstepping**
   - How do I earn trust from engineers who may resent a "new PM telling them what to build"?
   - How do I handle inheriting someone else's roadmap that I disagree with?
   - What quick wins actually work vs. which ones backfire?

3. **Month 2-3: Building momentum**
   - How do I start shaping the roadmap without causing political backlash?
   - When should I push back on leadership requests, and when should I absorb them?
   - How do I build alliances with design and engineering leads?

For each phase, tell me **one thing most new PMs do that veterans cringe at**, and what to do instead.

---

## Part 3: The Engineer-to-PM Translation Gaps

My engineering background is both my superpower and my blind spot. Explain in detail:

1. **Technical depth as a trap**: When does my engineering knowledge help, and when does it actively hurt my PM effectiveness? Give me specific scenarios where jumping into technical solutioning as a PM damages team dynamics, and how to channel that instinct productively.

2. **The estimation problem**: Engineers hate being asked for estimates. I know this. But now I need estimates from engineers. How do veteran PMs get reliable estimates without destroying trust? What techniques actually work beyond "add 2x buffer"?

3. **The "build vs. buy" instinct shift**: As an engineer, I default to "we can build that." As a PM, when should I fight for buy/integrate/partner instead? What is the real decision framework (not the one in MBA textbooks)?

4. **Thinking in outcomes, not outputs**: I am trained to think in shipped features and code quality. How do I rewire my brain to think in user outcomes and business metrics? Give me practical exercises or daily habits that force this shift.

5. **The communication mode switch**: Engineers communicate in precision and caveats. PMs communicate in narratives and conviction. How do I learn to present with confidence while my engineering brain is screaming "but there are seventeen edge cases"?

---

## Part 4: The Real PM Toolkit (Not the Theoretical One)

For each of the following PM activities, describe: (a) what the textbook says, (b) what actually happens in practice, and (c) the workaround or real technique that experienced PMs use.

1. **Writing PRDs / product specs**
2. **Running sprint planning and backlog grooming**
3. **Conducting user research on a tight budget and timeline**
4. **Prioritization frameworks (RICE, MoSCoW, etc.) in practice**
5. **Stakeholder management when stakeholders contradict each other**
6. **Saying "no" to the CEO or founder**
7. **Navigating a product pivot or major strategy change**
8. **Dealing with a launch that went poorly**
9. **OKRs and success metrics -- making them actually useful rather than performative**
10. **Cross-functional syncs -- running meetings that do not waste everyone's time**

---

## Part 5: Political Survival and Organizational Dynamics

This is the part no PM course teaches. Explain:

1. **How decisions actually get made** in most tech organizations (hint: it is rarely in the meeting where you think it happens).
2. **How to handle credit and blame**: The PM gets blamed when things fail and rarely gets credit when things succeed. How do veterans navigate this psychologically and politically?
3. **The art of pre-alignment**: How do you ensure that by the time a decision reaches a meeting, the outcome is already determined? Walk me through the mechanics of building consensus before the room.
4. **Detecting and surviving reorgs**: What are the early signals, and how do you position yourself?
5. **Managing conflict between engineering and design**: When they disagree fundamentally, what does the PM actually do? Not the platitude ("facilitate a conversation"), but the real moves.
6. **When to escalate vs. when to absorb**: A framework for knowing when to bring problems to leadership vs. handling them quietly.

---

## Part 6: Common PM Failure Modes (Especially for Ex-Engineers)

Describe the top 10 ways engineer-turned-PMs fail in their first year. For each failure mode:
- Describe what it looks like from the outside
- Explain the internal reasoning that leads to it
- Give me the specific correction

Be brutally honest. I would rather hear hard truths now than learn them by failing publicly.

---

## Part 7: The Accelerated Learning Plan

Given that I have approximately 4 weeks before starting, design a week-by-week learning plan that prioritizes the highest-leverage activities. For each week:

- **What to read** (specific books, essays, or resources -- not generic "read about PM" advice)
- **What to practice** (exercises I can do alone or with a friend)
- **What to observe** (if I have access to any product team, what should I watch for?)
- **What to write** (practice artifacts like mock PRDs, strategy docs, etc.)

Optimize this plan for someone who learns by doing, not by reading. Lean into my engineering strengths wherever possible.

---

## Part 8: Questions I Should Be Asking (But Do Not Know to Ask)

Finally, give me 15-20 questions that I do not even know I should be asking about product management. These should be the "unknown unknowns" -- the topics that surprised experienced PMs when they first encountered them, the gaps in understanding that only become visible after making mistakes. For each question, briefly explain why it matters.

---

## Output Format Instructions

- Use concrete, specific examples rather than abstract advice.
- Where possible, use the framing: "What a new PM thinks: [X]. What actually happens: [Y]. What a veteran does: [Z]."
- Do not hedge or soften difficult truths. Be direct.
- If a topic requires nuance based on company size (startup vs. large enterprise), call that out explicitly.
- When referencing frameworks or techniques, include enough detail that I can actually use them immediately, not just recognize the name.
- Total response should be comprehensive -- this is my crash course. Depth over brevity.
